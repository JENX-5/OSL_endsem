Section 1 — Foundation

Disk Anatomy & Why Scheduling Matters

🏢 The Office Building Analogy

Imagine a security guard (read/write head) in a skyscraper (disk platter). Requests come in from different floors (tracks). The guard can only serve one floor at a time and must physically travel between floors. A naive guard serves requests in arrival order — travelling 1→50→3→48→5. A smart guard plans the route to minimise total travel. That planning is disk scheduling.

Every extra track the head travels costs ~5–15ms. With thousands of I/O requests per second, the scheduling algorithm has a massive impact on system performance.

Disk Structure

Platter

Circular magnetic disk coated with magnetisable material. Multiple platters stack on a common spindle. Data is read/written magnetically.

Track & Cylinder

Concentric circles on a platter surface = tracks. The set of tracks at the same arm position across all platters = a cylinder. Numbered 0 to N−1.

Read/Write Head

Tiny electromagnetic coil that flies just above the platter surface. All heads move together as one arm assembly. One active head per platter surface.

Sector

Smallest addressable unit on a disk. A track is divided into sectors (typically 512 bytes or 4096 bytes). Data is read/written a sector at a time.

Disk Arm

The mechanical assembly that moves all heads in unison across the platter surfaces, positioning them over the correct cylinder for access.

Section 2 — Foundation

Seek Time, Latency & Total Head Movement

Three components make up the total time for a disk access:

1. Seek Time

Time for the arm to move to the correct cylinder (track). The dominant cost. Typically 3–15ms. What we minimise with scheduling.

2. Rotational Latency

Time waiting for the disk to rotate until the correct sector is under the head. Avg = half a rotation. ~4ms at 7200 RPM.

3. Transfer Time

Time to actually read or write the data once head is in position. Usually the fastest of the three. Depends on data size and rotation speed.

Total Head Movement (THM)

All scheduling algorithms in this lab are evaluated by their Total Head Movement — the sum of absolute distances the head travels to service all requests. Lower THM = better performance.

THM = Σ |cylinder[i+1] − cylinder[i]| for each consecutive pair in service order

All numerical examples use: 200 cylinders (0–199), head starts at cylinder 53, previous position 125 (used for directional algorithms), queue = 98, 183, 37, 122, 14, 124, 65, 67

Section 3 — Algorithms

FCFS — First Come, First Served

Simplest
First Come, First Served

Service requests strictly in the order they arrive in the queue. No reordering whatsoever. Fair to all processes but causes wild head swings across the disk.

How it Works

Maintain a FIFO queue of cylinder requests. The head visits them in exactly that order, regardless of distance. Simple to implement; no starvation.

  • Start at the current head position.
  • Dequeue the next request from the front of the queue.
  • Move the head to that cylinder. Add |distance| to THM.
  • Repeat until all requests are serviced.

Lab Manual Calculation

Start: 53. Queue: 98, 183, 37, 122, 14, 124, 65, 67

53 →98(45) →183(85) →37(146) →122(85) →14(108) →124(110) →65(59) →67(2)
THM = 45+85+146+85+108+110+59+2 = 640 cylinders

Problem with FCFS

Wild swings from cylinder 183 back to 37 (146 cylinders!) happen because requests are not sorted. FCFS treats the disk like a tape — completely ignoring the physical cost of movement. Only suitable for very light disk loads.

Section 4 — Algorithms

SSTF — Shortest Seek Time First

Greedy
Shortest Seek Time First

At each step, service the pending request that is closest to the current head position. A greedy nearest-neighbour strategy. Much better than FCFS but can cause starvation.

How it Works

After each request is serviced, scan the entire pending queue and pick the request with minimum |current − request| distance. Move there, repeat.

  • Start at current head position (53).
  • Scan all pending requests. Find the one closest to current position.
  • Move to that request. Add distance to THM. Remove from queue.
  • Repeat from step 2 until queue is empty.

Section 5 — Algorithms

SCAN — The Elevator Algorithm

Elevator
SCAN (Elevator Algorithm)

The head sweeps from one end of the disk to the other, servicing requests in its path. At each end, it reverses direction. Like an elevator — goes up serving, goes down serving, up again…

How it Works

The head moves in one direction, services all requests it passes. When it reaches the end (cylinder 0 or 199), it reverses and sweeps back. Requires knowing current direction of movement.

  • Know direction: moving towards lower or higher cylinders? (Determined from previous position — 125 → 53 means moving toward lower.)
  • Move in current direction, servicing all requests encountered.
  • Reach an end (cylinder 0 or 199) — reverse direction even if no pending requests there.
  • Continue servicing requests in new direction until all done.

Lab Manual Calculation

Start: 53. Previous: 125 → moving toward lower cylinders (decreasing). Queue sorted: 14, 37, 65, 67, 98, 122, 124, 183.

Direction: ↓ toward 0 first, then ↑.

53 ↓37(16) ↓14(23) ↓0(14) end ↑65(65) ↑67(2) ↑98(31) ↑122(24) ↑124(2) ↑183(59)
THM = 16+23+14+65+2+31+24+2+59 = 236 cylinders

Note on Direction

The starting direction is determined by the previous head position. Previous=125, current=53 → head was moving toward lower cylinders, so SCAN continues downward first. The end of disk (cylinder 0) is always visited even if no requests are there.

Section 6 — Algorithms

C-SCAN — Circular SCAN

Uniform Wait
Circular SCAN

Like SCAN, but the head only services requests in one direction. When it reaches the far end, it immediately jumps back to the beginning without servicing on the return. Provides more uniform wait times.

How it Works

The head moves in one direction (say, toward higher cylinders), servicing requests. When it reaches cylinder 199, it zips back to cylinder 0 without servicing anything, then sweeps upward again. Treats the disk as a circular buffer.

  • Move toward higher cylinders, service all requests in path.
  • Reach cylinder 199 — immediately jump to cylinder 0 (this jump is NOT counted in THM).
  • Continue sweeping toward higher cylinders again, servicing remaining requests.
  • Repeat until all requests serviced.

Lab Manual Calculation

Start: 53. Direction: ↑ toward higher cylinders. Queue sorted: 14, 37, 65, 67, 98, 122, 124, 183.

53 ↑65(12) ↑67(2) ↑98(31) ↑122(24) ↑124(2) ↑183(59) ↑199(16) ⟵ jump 0 ↑14(14) ↑37(23)
THM = 12+2+31+24+2+59+16+14+23 = 183 cylinders

Why C-SCAN beats SCAN for fairness

In SCAN, requests just behind the head must wait for a full sweep-and-return. In C-SCAN, the return is instant, so waiting time is bounded to one sweep. Useful for real-time and multimedia applications where predictable latency matters.

Important: Jump is NOT counted in THM

The jump from 199 back to 0 (or from 183 back to 14 in C-LOOK) represents the head being repositioned, not a data access. Exam questions often try to catch students adding this distance. Do not count it.

Section 7 — Algorithms

LOOK

Smarter SCAN
LOOK

Like SCAN, but the head doesn't travel all the way to the end of the disk. It only goes as far as the last request in each direction, then reverses. No wasted travel to cylinder 0 or 199 if no requests are there.

LOOK vs SCAN

SCAN

Always travels to physical end of disk (cylinder 0 or 199) before reversing, even if the last request is at cylinder 14.

LOOK

Travels only to the last pending request in each direction before reversing. Saves the unnecessary travel to the disk edge.

Lab Manual Calculation

Start: 53. Moving toward lower first (previous=125). Lowest request: 14, highest: 183.

53 ↓37(16) ↓14(23) ← reverses here ↑65(51) ↑67(2) ↑98(31) ↑122(24) ↑124(2) ↑183(59)
THM = 16+23+51+2+31+24+2+59 = 208 cylinders

Saves 28 cylinders vs SCAN (236) — the unnecessary travel from 14→0 and back is eliminated.

Section 8 — Algorithms

C-LOOK — Circular LOOK

Most Efficient
C-LOOK

Combines the best of C-SCAN and LOOK. Moves in one direction, goes only to the last request (not disk end), then jumps back to the first request (not cylinder 0). The most efficient algorithm in this set.

C-LOOK vs C-SCAN

C-SCAN

Goes to physical end (199), jumps back to cylinder 0. Wastes travel if last request is at 183 and first is at 14.

C-LOOK

Goes to last request (183), jumps to first request (14). Saves all unnecessary travel beyond the actual data range.

Lab Manual Calculation

Start: 53. Direction: ↑. Highest request: 183, lowest: 14.

53 ↑65(12) ↑67(2) ↑98(31) ↑122(24) ↑124(2) ↑183(59) ⟵ jump 14 ↑37(23)
THM = 12+2+31+24+2+59+23 = 153 cylinders

Best result among all 6 algorithms for this example. 183→14 jump is NOT counted.

Which Algorithm Should You Use?

Use SSTF when…

Throughput matters most and requests cluster around a few cylinders. Common in lightly loaded desktop systems.

Use SCAN/LOOK when…

Medium load, need to prevent starvation. LOOK is preferred over SCAN (no wasted head travel to disk ends).

Use C-SCAN/C-LOOK when…

Uniform wait time is critical — databases, servers with many concurrent I/O requests. C-LOOK is the most efficient variant.

Section 12 — Implementation

C Code Implementation

C 
/* FCFS Disk Scheduling — serve in arrival order */
#include <stdio.h>
#include <stdlib.h>

int fcfs(int queue[], int n, int head) {
    int thm = 0, cur = head;
    printf("FCFS: %d", head);
    for(int i = 0; i < n; i++) {
        thm += abs(queue[i] - cur);   /* add seek distance */
        cur  = queue[i];
        printf(" → %d", cur);
    }
    printf("\nTHM = %d\n", thm);
    return thm;
}

int main() {
    int q[] = {98,183,37,122,14,124,65,67};
    int n   = sizeof(q)/sizeof(q[0]);
    fcfs(q, n, 53);   /* expected: 640 */
    return 0;
}
C 
/* SSTF Disk Scheduling — always pick closest request */
#include <stdio.h>
#include <stdlib.h>
#define INF 99999

int sstf(int queue[], int n, int head) {
    int visited[n], thm = 0, cur = head;
    for(int i=0;i<n;i++) visited[i]=0;

    printf("SSTF: %d", head);
    for(int count = 0; count < n; count++) {
        int minDist = INF, pick = -1;

        /* Find unvisited request closest to current position */
        for(int i = 0; i < n; i++) {
            if(!visited[i] && abs(queue[i]-cur) < minDist) {
                minDist = abs(queue[i]-cur);
                pick    = i;
            }
        }
        visited[pick] = 1;
        thm += abs(queue[pick] - cur);
        cur  = queue[pick];
        printf(" → %d", cur);
    }
    printf("\nTHM = %d\n", thm);
    return thm;
}

int main() {
    int q[] = {98,183,37,122,14,124,65,67};
    int n   = sizeof(q)/sizeof(q[0]);
    sstf(q, n, 53);   /* expected: 236 */
    return 0;
}

/* SCAN (Elevator) Disk Scheduling
 * dir: 0 = moving toward lower, 1 = moving toward higher
 * Always goes to disk end (0 or maxCyl-1) before reversing */
#include <stdio.h>
#include <stdlib.h>
#include <string.h>

int cmp(const void*a,const void*b){return*(ty int*)a - *(ty int*)b;}

int scan(int queue[], int n, int head, int dir, int maxCyl) {
    int sorted[n+1], thm = 0, cur = head;
    for(int i=0;i<n;i++) sorted[i]=queue[i];
    qsort(sorted, n, sizeof(int), cmp);

    printf("SCAN: %d", head);

    if(dir == 0) {
        /* Moving toward lower cylinders first */
        for(int i = n-1; i >= 0; i--)     /* service requests below head */
            if(sorted[i] <= head) {
                thm += abs(sorted[i]-cur); cur=sorted[i]; printf(" → %d",cur);
            }
        /* Go to cylinder 0 */
        thm += cur; printf(" → 0"); cur = 0;
        for(int i = 0; i < n; i++)           /* service requests above head */
            if(sorted[i] > head) {
                thm += abs(sorted[i]-cur); cur=sorted[i]; printf(" → %d",cur);
            }
    } else {
        /* Moving toward higher cylinders first */
        for(int i = 0; i < n; i++)
            if(sorted[i] >= head) {
                thm += abs(sorted[i]-cur); cur=sorted[i]; printf(" → %d",cur);
            }
        thm += (maxCyl-1-cur); printf(" → %d",maxCyl-1); cur=maxCyl-1;
        for(int i = n-1; i >= 0; i--)
            if(sorted[i] < head) {
                thm += abs(sorted[i]-cur); cur=sorted[i]; printf(" → %d",cur);
            }
    }
    printf("\nTHM = %d\n", thm);
    return thm;
}

int main() {
    int q[] = {98,183,37,122,14,124,65,67};
    int n = sizeof(q)/sizeof(q[0]);
    scan(q, n, 53, 0, 200); /* dir=0: moving toward 0 first → THM=236 */
    return 0;
}

/* C-SCAN: moves in one direction only, jumps back at end */
#include <stdio.h>
#include <stdlib.h>

int cmp(const void*a,const void*b){return*(int*)a-*(int*)b;}

int cscan(int q[], int n, int head, int maxCyl) {
    int sorted[n], thm = 0, cur = head;
    for(int i=0;i<n;i++) sorted[i]=q[i];
    qsort(sorted, n, sizeof(int), cmp);

    printf("C-SCAN: %d", head);

    /* Phase 1: service all requests >= head (moving up) */
    for(int i = 0; i < n; i++)
        if(sorted[i] >= head) {
            thm += abs(sorted[i]-cur); cur=sorted[i];
            printf(" → %d",cur);
        }

    /* Reach end of disk */
    thm += (maxCyl-1) - cur;
    printf(" → %d", maxCyl-1);

    /* Jump to cylinder 0 — NOT counted in THM */
    printf(" [jump→0]"); cur = 0;

    /* Phase 2: service remaining requests < head */
    for(int i = 0; i < n; i++)
        if(sorted[i] < head) {
            thm += abs(sorted[i]-cur); cur=sorted[i];
            printf(" → %d",cur);
        }

    printf("\nTHM = %d\n", thm);
    return thm;
}

int main() {
    int q[] = {98,183,37,122,14,124,65,67};
    int n   = sizeof(q)/sizeof(q[0]);
    cscan(q, n, 53, 200);  /* expected: 183 */
    return 0;
}

#include <stdlib.h>

int look(int q[], int n, int head, int dir) {

    for(int i=0;i<n;i++) sorted[i]=q[i];

            if(sorted[i]>=head){thm+=abs(sorted[i]-cur);cur=sorted[i];printf(" → %d",cur);}

Section 13 — Exam Prep

Common Mistakes

✗
Counting the C-SCAN/C-LOOK jump in THM
The return journey (199→0 in C-SCAN, or 183→14 in C-LOOK) is NOT counted in THM. It's a repositioning, not a seek. Students who add this jump get wrong answers on every C-SCAN/C-LOOK calculation.
✗
Not knowing the starting direction for SCAN/LOOK
The direction is always inferred from the previous head position. Previous=125, current=53 → head was moving toward lower cylinders. If the problem doesn't give previous position, assume given direction or state your assumption.
✗
SCAN vs LOOK confusion at the ends
SCAN always travels to cylinder 0 or 199 before reversing, even if no request is there (adds wasted travel). LOOK only goes to the last actual request before reversing. In the lab example, SCAN adds 14 cylinders (14→0) that LOOK saves.
✗
SSTF starvation claim — "it's always bad"
SSTF can starve requests but gives excellent average seek time for moderate loads. It's not "bad" — it's a valid tradeoff. SCAN-family algorithms prevent starvation but have higher average seek times under light loads.
✗
SSTF greedily choosing wrong cylinder when distances tie
When two requests are equidistant, pick either one (typically the one in the current direction of movement, or the lower-numbered one). State your tiebreaking rule in exam answers.
✓
THM formula is always a sum of absolute differences
THM = Σ|next − current|. You never subtract — all terms are positive. If you get a negative intermediate value, you've made an ordering mistake.
Section 14 — Exam Prep

Viva / Exam Questions

Short Answer

What is seek time and why do we minimise it? ▾
Seek time is the time for the disk arm to move to the required cylinder (track). It is the dominant component of disk access time (3–15ms), far larger than rotational latency (~4ms) or transfer time. Minimising seek time directly reduces I/O response time and increases throughput.
What is Total Head Movement (THM)? ▾
THM is the sum of all absolute distances the disk head travels to service all pending requests in a given queue. It is the standard metric for comparing disk scheduling algorithms. Formula: THM = Σ|cylinder[i+1] − cylinder[i]| for each consecutive pair in the service order. Lower THM = better performance.
Why can SSTF cause starvation? Give an example. ▾
SSTF always picks the closest request. If requests cluster near cylinder 50 and a request arrives at cylinder 190, it might never get served — every time the head finishes a close request, another close one arrives, keeping the head near 50 indefinitely. This is starvation: the far request waits forever. Fix: add an aging mechanism or switch to SCAN-family.
What is the difference between SCAN and C-SCAN? ▾
SCAN sweeps to the disk end, then reverses and services requests on the return trip too. C-SCAN sweeps to the disk end, then immediately jumps back to the beginning (without servicing on return), then sweeps again. C-SCAN provides more uniform waiting times because requests near the beginning of the disk don't have to wait for a full sweep-and-return — they're served on the very next upward sweep.
What is the difference between SCAN and LOOK? ▾
SCAN travels all the way to the physical end of the disk (cylinder 0 or N−1) before reversing. LOOK only travels to the last pending request before reversing. LOOK is more efficient — it avoids wasted head movement beyond the last request. In the lab example, LOOK saves 28 cylinders compared to SCAN (208 vs 236).

Trick / Long Questions

🎯 TRICK: SSTF and SCAN give the same THM (236) in the lab example — does this mean they're equivalent? ▾
No — equal THM for one example doesn't mean equivalent algorithms. Key differences: (1) SSTF can cause starvation; SCAN cannot. (2) SSTF's THM varies widely with different queues and can be much worse than SCAN if the queue changes. (3) SCAN has more predictable worst-case behaviour. (4) Their service orders are completely different — the same THM is a numerical coincidence for this specific input.
🎯 When would FCFS actually be the best choice? ▾
FCFS is best (or at least acceptable) when: (1) The disk queue is very short — with 1–2 requests, all algorithms give the same result. (2) Requests naturally arrive in cylinder order already. (3) Fairness is paramount and response time doesn't matter. (4) In SSD/NVMe storage where there is no physical seek — all locations are accessed in equal time, making seek optimisation irrelevant. In practice, modern SSDs use FCFS or simple FIFO variants for this reason.
🎯 Why does C-LOOK give a lower THM than C-SCAN (153 vs 183)? ▾
C-SCAN always travels to the physical disk end (cylinder 199) and jumps from 0, even if the highest request is at 183 and the lowest is at 14. This wastes: (199−183)=16 cylinders going beyond the last request, and (14−0)=14 cylinders served from 0 instead of 14 — but wait, those 14 are counted. The key saving is (199−183)=16 cylinders of travel beyond the last request. C-LOOK doesn't go to 199 — it stops at 183 and jumps to 14 (the first request). So C-LOOK saves 16+14=30 cylinders? Actually 183−153=30. Exactly: C-LOOK saves travel to disk-end AND from cylinder-0 to first-request.
🎯 Does disk scheduling still matter for SSDs? ▾
Largely no for seek time — SSDs have no moving parts, so access time is uniform regardless of "cylinder." However: (1) SSDs still benefit from I/O scheduling for write coalescing and managing write amplification. (2) NCQ (Native Command Queuing) and NVMe queues still reorder requests, but for different reasons (parallelism, wear levelling) not seek optimisation. (3) HDD disk scheduling remains fully relevant for spinning disks, which are still common in bulk storage and servers.
Section 15 — Quick Revision

Summary Sheet

FCFS

  • Service in arrival order
  • No reordering — simplest
  • THM = 640 (lab example)
  • No starvation; worst performance
  • Good for SSD (no seek cost)

SSTF

  • Always pick nearest request
  • Greedy — great average THM
  • THM = 236 (lab example)
  • Can starve distant requests
  • No direction tracking needed

SCAN (Elevator)

  • Sweep to disk end, reverse
  • Services both directions
  • THM = 236 (lab example)
  • No starvation; needs direction
  • Uneven wait — far end waits longer

C-SCAN

  • One direction only, jump at end
  • Uniform waiting times
  • THM = 183 (lab example)
  • Jump (199→0) NOT counted
  • Good for servers/databases

LOOK

  • SCAN but no wasted end travel
  • Reverses at last request
  • THM = 208 (lab example)
  • Saves travel vs SCAN
  • LOOK ≡ Smarter SCAN

C-LOOK

  • C-SCAN but no wasted end travel
  • Jump to first request (not 0)
  • THM = 153 (lab example, best!)
  • Jump NOT counted in THM
  • Most efficient overall

THM Results (lab example)

  • FCFS: 640 (worst)
  • SSTF: 236
  • SCAN: 236
  • C-SCAN: 183
  • LOOK: 208
  • C-LOOK: 153 (best)

Key Rules

  • THM = Σ|next − current|
  • Direction from previous position
  • SCAN goes to 0/199 — LOOK does not
  • C-* jumps are never counted
  • Only SSTF can starve
